Task 1 — Assess Immediacy of Impact: Look at the cat picture and determine how quickly and involuntarily it provokes an emotional reaction. Consider whether the image delivers its emotional payload instantly — the kind of picture that makes a viewer laugh, gasp, or soften before they have consciously decided to feel anything. Evaluate whether the emotional content is front and center, seizing attention the moment the image registers, or whether it is buried, subtle, or dependent on prolonged study. A picture of a cat mid-leap with a face of pure alarm, or a kitten curled inside an oversized shoe, hits immediately and without explanation. A technically lovely portrait of a cat sitting normally does not. The task is to score how powerfully the image lands in that first half-second of viewing — the gut-punch moment — independent of whether the feeling persists or deepens afterward.

Task 2 — Assess Depth of Feeling: Examine the cat picture and determine whether the emotional response it provokes has weight and staying power beyond the initial moment of contact. Consider whether the image captures something that gestures toward larger human experiences — tenderness, vulnerability, innocence, companionship, aging, wonder — in a way that makes the viewer linger, return to the image, or find it surfacing in their mind unbidden later. A picture of an elderly cat resting peacefully in fading light, or a cat gently pressing its nose against a crying child's cheek, taps into something that outlasts the act of looking. The task is to score how deeply the image penetrates beneath the surface of a quick reaction, and how likely the feeling is to endure — not just whether the viewer reacts, but whether they remember.

Task 3 — Assess the Urge to Share: Evaluate the cat picture and determine how strongly it compels a viewer to show it to someone else. Consider whether the image carries an emotional charge so potent that experiencing it alone feels incomplete — the kind of picture that makes a person immediately reach for their phone to send to a friend, or nudge a companion and point at the screen. This is not about whether the image is broadly appealing in a generic sense, but whether it creates that specific, almost involuntary impulse to pass the feeling along. The task is to score the universality and transferability of the emotional response: the confidence that this image will not just move one particular viewer, but will reliably land for nearly anyone who sees it, making it too good to keep to oneself.
